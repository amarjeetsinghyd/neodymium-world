3D printing company Divergent to produce Tomahawk structure at new factory, CEO says

Published: Wed, 17 Jun 2026 14:03:28 +0000

Executive Summary

This intelligence report details the pivotal entry of Divergent Technologies into the defense manufacturing sector, specifically focusing on its contract to 3D print critical midbody structures for the Tomahawk cruise missile. The establishment of 'Factory 2,' a new 430,000-square-foot production facility in Long Beach, California, signifies a substantial expansion of advanced manufacturing capabilities within the U.S. defense industrial base. This strategic development is poised to significantly enhance supply chain resilience, accelerate production rates for crucial weapon systems, and potentially reduce per-unit costs through the application of Divergent's proprietary additive manufacturing technologies, notably its Monolith One series of 3D printers. The initiative reflects a broader trend toward leveraging emerging technologies to meet the escalating demands of national security, ensuring robust and diversified defense procurement pathways.

Technical Deep-Dive

The integration of Divergent Technologies into the defense supply chain represents a significant advancement in the application of Additive Manufacturing (AM), specifically in the realm of high-performance metallic structures for critical weapon systems. The core of this technical evolution lies within Divergent’s proprietary manufacturing ecosystem and its flagship hardware, the Monolith One 3D printer series, housed within the newly commissioned 'Factory 2.'

1. Advanced Additive Manufacturing Processes and Materials: Divergent's methodology is rooted in advanced powder bed fusion (PBF) additive manufacturing, a process where metal powder layers are selectively melted by a high-energy source. For the Tomahawk midbody structure, this likely involves materials such as high-strength aluminum alloys (e.g., AlSi10Mg), titanium alloys (e.g., Ti-6Al-4V), or specialized high-performance steels. These materials are chosen for their exceptional strength-to-weight ratios, corrosion resistance, and ability to withstand the extreme dynamic and thermal stresses inherent in missile flight. The 'highest laser power of any machine on the market,' as stated, indicates several potential technical advantages: * Increased Build Speed: Higher laser power translates directly to faster melting kinetics, allowing for quicker solidification of each layer and thus higher overall print speeds. This is critical for achieving the projected throughput of 30,000 missile airframes or 60,000 warhead casings annually. * Larger Build Volumes: Powerful lasers can process larger areas efficiently, potentially enabling the production of larger, monolithic components without the need for subsequent assembly, reducing part count and potential failure points. * Material Versatility: High power may also broaden the range of compatible materials, allowing for the processing of refractory metals or high-temperature alloys that require more energy to melt. * Improved Material Densification: Sufficient laser power ensures full densification of the material, minimizing porosity and maximizing mechanical properties, which is paramount for structural integrity in aerospace and defense applications.

2. Gas Flow and Powder Delivery Systems: Complementing the high-power lasers, Divergent's 'most advanced gas flow and powder delivery system' is crucial for print quality and material utilization. Key aspects include: * Inert Atmosphere Control: PBF processes for reactive metals (like titanium and aluminum) demand extremely controlled inert gas environments (e.g., argon or nitrogen) to prevent oxidation, contamination, and ensure consistent material properties. Advanced gas flow systems ensure uniform distribution and efficient removal of byproducts like spatter, maintaining a pristine build environment. * Precision Powder Re-coating: The powder delivery system must precisely and consistently spread thin, uniform layers of metal powder. Advanced systems often employ sophisticated roller or blade mechanisms coupled with real-time feedback sensors to ensure layer height accuracy and uniform powder density, which directly impacts part quality and geometric precision. * Closed-Loop Material Handling: For cost-effectiveness and safety, especially with fine metal powders, closed-loop systems for powder recycling and handling are essential. These minimize material waste, reduce contamination risks, and enhance operational efficiency.

3. The Monolith One Series and Production Throughput: With 64 Monolith One machines slated for Factory 2, the doubling of production throughput 'compared to other 3D printers' is a key metric. This is likely achieved through a combination of the aforementioned high-power lasers and optimized process parameters, potentially including multi-laser configurations or intelligent build strategy algorithms. The in-house development of these machines allows Divergent to tightly integrate hardware and software, tailoring the entire system for maximum efficiency and cost reduction ('lower cost per unit'). The 'very cost effective' nature, coupled with 'high reliability,' suggests robust industrial-grade design, automated process monitoring, and sophisticated quality control systems inherent to the machine's operation.

4. Factory 2 Infrastructure and Automation: The 430,000-square-foot 'Factory 2' in Long Beach is designed for scale and efficiency. This facility will integrate: * Robotics and Automation: Beyond the 3D printers themselves, the factory will leverage robotics for automated material handling (powder loading, part removal), post-processing (support structure removal, surface finishing), and quality inspection, reducing manual labor and potential human error. * Software Integration and Digital Thread: Divergent's broader 'Divergent Adaptive Production System' (DAPS) concept implies a comprehensive software suite that manages the entire production lifecycle, from design optimization (e.g., topology optimization for weight reduction in the Tomahawk structure), through print file generation, real-time machine monitoring, and quality data capture. This 'digital thread' ensures traceability and consistency. * Scalability and Flexibility: The factory's design, capable of pivoting between defense and commercial production (e.g., automotive), demonstrates inherent flexibility. This modularity allows for dynamic allocation of resources based on demand, ensuring high capital utilization and resilience against market fluctuations.

5. Tomahawk Midbody Structure Specifics: The Tomahawk midbody structure is a critical component, housing guidance electronics, fuel tanks, and connecting the missile's forward and aft sections. Additive manufacturing offers unique advantages here: * Weight Reduction: Complex internal lattice structures or optimized geometries, impossible with traditional manufacturing, can significantly reduce weight without compromising strength, directly impacting missile range or payload capacity. * Part Consolidation: AM can integrate multiple components into a single printed part, reducing assembly time, labor, and the potential for manufacturing defects or stress concentrations at joints. * Enhanced Performance: The ability to customize internal flow paths or integrate sensors directly into the structure opens possibilities for future performance enhancements and system integration.

6. Quality Assurance and Certification: For defense applications, rigorous quality assurance and certification processes are non-negotiable. This includes adherence to military specifications (MIL-SPECs), aerospace standards (e.g., AS9100), and stringent material qualification. Divergent will undoubtedly employ advanced Non-Destructive Testing (NDT) methods such as X-ray computed tomography (CT scanning), ultrasonic inspection, and eddy current testing to ensure structural integrity, material consistency, and freedom from defects within every printed component.

Strategic Impact

The integration of Divergent Technologies as a second-source supplier for the Tomahawk cruise missile midbody structure carries profound strategic implications across several critical domains:

1. Enhanced Supply Chain Resilience and Diversification: The U.S. defense industrial base has faced challenges concerning single points of failure and bottlenecks, particularly in times of heightened demand or geopolitical instability. Divergent's entry significantly diversifies Raytheon's supply chain for a critical component of the Tomahawk missile, mitigating risks associated with reliance on a sole manufacturer. This creates redundancy, strengthens the industrial base against disruptions, and ensures a more robust and responsive supply of essential weapon systems.

2. Accelerated Production Capabilities and Readiness: The projected eightfold increase in annual production capacity, translating to upward of 30,000 missile airframes or 60,000 warhead casings annually from Factory 2, directly addresses the urgent need for faster production ramp-up. In an era of great power competition and potential peer-level conflict, the ability to rapidly replenish strategic munition stockpiles and meet surge requirements is paramount for maintaining deterrence and ensuring military readiness. This capacity is critical for sustaining prolonged operations or quickly responding to emerging threats.

3. Cost Reduction and Affordability: The 'lower cost per unit' achieved through Divergent's advanced additive manufacturing processes is a significant strategic advantage. For large-scale procurement programs like the Tomahawk, even marginal per-unit cost reductions can result in substantial savings over the lifecycle of the weapon system. This enhanced affordability allows the Department of Defense to acquire more assets within budget constraints, potentially freeing up funds for other critical defense investments or increasing the overall inventory of critical munitions.

4. Modernization of the Defense Industrial Base: Divergent's commitment to leveraging '3D printing, robotics and software' for component manufacturing signals a strategic shift towards adopting advanced manufacturing technologies across the defense sector. This serves as a critical model for modernizing the broader industrial base, encouraging innovation, and fostering the development of next-generation manufacturing capabilities that are agile, efficient, and responsive to evolving defense needs. This modernization is vital for maintaining a technological edge over adversaries.

5. Technological Superiority and Performance Enhancements: The ability to produce complex, optimized structures through additive manufacturing opens avenues for future performance enhancements of weapon systems. Lighter, stronger, or functionally integrated components can lead to increased range, improved payload capacity, or enhanced survivability for missiles like the Tomahawk. This continuous technological improvement is crucial for sustaining qualitative military superiority.

6. Dual-Use Capabilities and Economic Leverage: The flexibility of Factory 2 to produce both defense and commercial (e.g., automotive) components highlights a strategic dual-use capability. This allows for diversified revenue streams, reducing the defense sector's sole reliance on government contracts and fostering cross-sector innovation. The ability to pivot between markets enhances capital utilization and makes defense manufacturing more economically sustainable and attractive for private investment, strengthening the overall national economic base supporting defense.

7. Geopolitical and Global Posture Implications: A robust and agile domestic manufacturing capability for critical munitions strengthens the U.S.'s geopolitical standing. It underscores self-sufficiency and reduces reliance on potentially vulnerable international supply chains. The capacity to quickly scale production sends a clear signal to potential adversaries regarding the nation's resolve and capability to sustain prolonged military efforts, thereby contributing to strategic deterrence and stability.

Conclusion

The strategic partnership between Divergent Technologies and Raytheon for the 3D printing of Tomahawk cruise missile midbody structures marks a seminal development in the ongoing modernization of the U.S. defense industrial base. The establishment of 'Factory 2' and the deployment of advanced Monolith One additive manufacturing systems represent not merely an increase in production capacity, but a fundamental transformation in how critical components are conceptualized, produced, and integrated into national security assets. This initiative directly addresses critical imperatives for supply chain resilience, rapid production scalability, and cost efficiency, all of which are paramount in the current complex geopolitical landscape.

The profound technical capabilities, exemplified by high-power lasers, sophisticated gas flow, and advanced powder delivery systems, are set to deliver a significant increase in throughput and a reduction in per-unit cost, making advanced weapon systems more attainable and sustainable for large-scale deployment. Furthermore, Divergent's dual-use manufacturing strategy provides a robust economic model that fosters innovation across defense and commercial sectors, thereby strengthening the national technological ecosystem.

In essence, Divergent's pivotal role signifies a deliberate and impactful shift towards integrating cutting-edge additive manufacturing into core defense procurement. This trajectory is expected to yield substantial benefits in terms of strategic readiness, technological superiority, and the overall robustness of the nation's defense capabilities, positioning the United States to better meet future security challenges and maintain its strategic advantage on the global stage. This development underscores the transformative potential of advanced manufacturing to redefine the paradigms of defense production for decades to come.
