Pentagon may ‘sacrifice’ traditional weapons to buy more drones if reconciliation fails: CTO

Published: Fri, 12 Jun 2026 19:28:59 +0000

Executive Summary

This report addresses the critical strategic inflection point currently confronting the Department of Defense (DoD), as articulated by Emil Michael, Chief Technology Officer (CTO), regarding potential resource reallocation from traditional, 'exquisite' weapons systems towards low-cost autonomous weapons platforms. The impetus for this re-evaluation is rooted in a complex interplay of fiscal pressures, evolving geopolitical landscapes, and the accelerating pace of technological advancement, particularly in artificial intelligence and robotics. The CTO's statement underscores a pivotal policy dilemma: whether to continue prioritizing the development and procurement of high-cost, multi-generational platforms or to significantly invest in expendable, networked autonomous systems that offer advantages in mass, resilience, and cost-effectiveness. The potential failure of legislative or budgetary reconciliation mechanisms would likely exacerbate existing fiscal constraints, thereby accelerating the necessity for such profound trade-offs. This report provides a detailed analysis of the technical distinctions between these two paradigms, assesses the strategic implications of a shift in procurement priorities, and outlines the critical factors influencing the DoD's future force structure and operational doctrine.

Technical Deep-Dive

The strategic re-evaluation articulated by the DoD's CTO necessitates an exceptionally detailed technical examination of the two distinct weapon system paradigms: 'exquisite' traditional platforms and 'low-cost autonomous weapons.' Understanding the inherent characteristics, operational profiles, and technological underpinnings of each is crucial for comprehending the profound implications of potential trade-offs.

I. Exquisite Traditional Weapons Systems: These systems represent the apex of conventional military engineering, characterized by their unparalleled individual performance, multi-role capabilities, and significant investment in research, development, and procurement. They are designed for superior performance across various metrics, often at substantial per-unit cost and operational expense.

  • Definition and Examples: This category encompasses flagship platforms such as:

    • Fifth and Sixth-Generation Fighter Aircraft: Examples include the Lockheed Martin F-35 Lightning II, the F-22 Raptor, and emerging Next-Generation Air Dominance (NGAD) platforms. These aircraft integrate advanced stealth technology, sophisticated sensor fusion, networked data links, and multi-spectral electronic warfare capabilities. They are designed for air superiority, precision strike, and advanced intelligence, surveillance, and reconnaissance (ISR) missions.
    • Aircraft Carriers and Associated Strike Groups: Nimitz and Gerald R. Ford-class nuclear-powered aircraft carriers, alongside their escorting destroyers (e.g., Arleigh Burke-class), cruisers (e.g., Ticonderoga-class), and submarines. These are mobile sovereign territories designed for global power projection, sustained operations, and multi-domain warfare coordination.
    • Strategic Bombers: The Northrop Grumman B-2 Spirit and the forthcoming B-21 Raider represent ultra-low observable platforms capable of delivering conventional and nuclear payloads over intercontinental distances.
    • Advanced Precision-Guided Munitions (PGMs): Systems like the Joint Air-to-Surface Standoff Missile (JASSM), Joint Direct Attack Munition (JDAM), and Standard Missile-6 (SM-6) are highly sophisticated, often incorporating advanced navigation, guidance, and terminal-guidance technologies to achieve exceptional accuracy and lethality against high-value targets.
    • Advanced Satellite Systems: Multi-billion dollar constellations providing global communication, navigation (GPS), missile warning, and ISR capabilities, featuring hardened electronics and anti-jamming measures.

  • Technical Characteristics:

    • High Unit Cost and Development Cycle: Development can span decades, involving immense R&D budgets and significant capital expenditure per unit (e.g., F-35 unit cost exceeding $100 million, carrier costs in the tens of billions).
    • Complex Sub-Systems Integration: Deep integration of highly specialized sensors (AESA radars, IRST, EW suites), processing units, flight control systems, and weapon release mechanisms.
    • Advanced Materials and Manufacturing: Extensive use of stealth-enabling materials (radar-absorbent coatings), advanced composites, and precision manufacturing techniques to achieve low observable signatures, high strength-to-weight ratios, and extreme performance.
    • Reliance on Highly Trained Human Operators: Requires extensive and continuous training for pilots, crew, and maintenance personnel, representing a substantial human capital investment.
    • Limited Production Numbers: Due to cost and complexity, these systems are produced in relatively smaller quantities, emphasizing individual platform superiority over numerical advantage.
    • Maintenance and Logistical Tail: Demanding maintenance schedules, specialized tools, spare parts logistics, and dedicated infrastructure contribute to high lifecycle costs.

  • Strengths: Unmatched individual capability, global reach, deterrent value, established interoperability within existing command and control (C2) structures, and the ability to operate in contested environments due to advanced stealth and electronic warfare.

  • Weaknesses: Susceptibility to attrition in high-intensity conflicts (high cost of loss), long lead times for replacement, fiscal unsustainability for rapid scaling, vulnerability to saturation attacks from numerous low-cost threats, and potential for technological overmatch by adversaries leveraging asymmetric strategies.

II. Low-Cost Autonomous Weapons (Drones/Unmanned Systems): This paradigm emphasizes numerical superiority, distributed functionality, expendability, and potentially lower cognitive burden on human operators. These systems leverage advancements in artificial intelligence, robotics, miniaturization, and networked communications.

  • Definition and Examples: This category is broad and rapidly expanding, including:

    • Small-to-Medium Unmanned Aerial Systems (UAS): Commercial off-the-shelf (COTS) drones adapted for military use (e.g., DJI Mavic series), loitering munitions ('kamikaze drones' like Switchblade, Shahed-136), and tactical reconnaissance drones. These can be hand-launched or deployed from small vehicles.
    • Large ISR/Strike UAS: MQ-9 Reaper, RQ-4 Global Hawk, and their successors. While not 'low-cost' in the same sense as smaller drones, they offer significantly lower operational costs and reduced risk to human life compared to manned aircraft for persistent ISR and precision strike missions.
    • Swarming Drones: Collections of numerous small, networked UAS designed to operate collaboratively to overwhelm defenses, conduct distributed ISR, or engage multiple targets simultaneously. Project Maven and the Replicator initiative are examples of DoD focus here.
    • Unmanned Ground Vehicles (UGVs): Autonomous or semi-autonomous robots for reconnaissance, logistics (e.g., RCV-L, RCV-M programs), mine clearance, and even direct combat roles. Examples include robotic mules, armed robotic combat vehicles.
    • Unmanned Surface Vessels (USVs) and Unmanned Undersea Vehicles (UUVs): Autonomous naval platforms for ISR, anti-submarine warfare (ASW), mine countermeasures (MCM), and potentially strike missions. Examples include the Sea Hunter USV and various UUV programs.

  • Technical Characteristics:

    • Lower Unit Cost and Rapid Development/Procurement: Can range from hundreds to millions of dollars per unit, orders of magnitude less than traditional systems. Development cycles are often months or a few years, leveraging agile development and COTS components.
    • Modularity and Adaptability: Often designed with modular payloads, allowing for rapid reconfiguration for different missions (e.g., ISR, electronic warfare, strike) and easy integration of new technologies.
    • Expendability/Attrition Tolerance: The relatively low cost allows for operational concepts where losses are anticipated and acceptable, enabling higher-risk missions and saturation tactics.
    • Autonomy Levels: Ranges from tele-operated (human-in-the-loop) to semi-autonomous (human-on-the-loop for critical decisions) to potentially fully autonomous systems (human-out-of-the-loop for specific tasks). This involves sophisticated AI for navigation, target recognition, decision-making, and collective behavior.
    • Networked Operations: Emphasis on robust, resilient, and potentially self-healing communication networks to enable collaborative operations, data sharing, and distributed C2 across large numbers of platforms.
    • Simpler Manufacturing: Can leverage commercial manufacturing processes, additive manufacturing (3D printing), and potentially be produced at much higher volumes and faster rates.
    • Cognitive Overmatch Potential: AI-driven systems can process vast amounts of data, make decisions at machine speed, and execute complex maneuvers that would overwhelm human operators or traditional systems in a coordinated swarm attack.

  • Strengths: Mass production potential, reduced risk to human life, cognitive and physical overmatch through numerical superiority ('swarming'), persistent ISR at lower cost, precision strike with expendable assets, adaptability to diverse mission sets, rapid technological refresh, and potential for disaggregated force projection.

  • Weaknesses: Vulnerability to electronic warfare (jamming, spoofing), cyberattack (hacking, data corruption), and kinetic interception (though at lower cost). Ethical and legal challenges regarding autonomy in lethal decision-making. Dependence on robust, resilient communication links (unless highly autonomous). Potential for 'sensor blindness' or misidentification with current AI. Integration challenges with legacy C2 structures and doctrines.

III. The Trade-Off Mechanism and Rationale:

The central 'trade-off' is driven by several converging factors:

  • Fiscal Realities: Flat or declining defense budgets, coupled with inflationary pressures and the high fixed costs of personnel and legacy systems, necessitate difficult choices. The opportunity cost of developing one exquisite system means foregoing investment in numerous autonomous platforms.
  • Strategic Imperatives: The rise of peer and near-peer adversaries (e.g., China, Russia) employing sophisticated Anti-Access/Area Denial (A2/AD) strategies challenges the survivability and effectiveness of traditional, high-value platforms. A large volume of low-cost, expendable systems can potentially penetrate or saturate such defenses more effectively.
  • Operational Doctrine Shift: There is a growing doctrinal shift towards 'affordable mass,' 'distributed lethality,' and 'resilient C2.' This envisions a future battlespace where distributed networks of autonomous systems complement or, in some scenarios, replace the concentrated power of exquisite platforms. The goal is to impose disproportionate costs on adversaries, forcing them to expend high-value interceptors against low-cost targets.
  • Pacing the Threat and Innovation: Autonomous systems, with their shorter development cycles and modularity, offer a faster pathway to integrate emerging technologies (e.g., advanced AI algorithms, new sensor types, energy sources) and adapt to rapidly evolving threats. This iterative approach contrasts with the long, rigid development cycles of exquisite systems.
  • Human Capital Optimization: Shifting certain 'dull, dirty, and dangerous' missions to autonomous systems can free up highly trained human personnel for more complex decision-making, strategic planning, or specialized tasks where human judgment is irreplaceable. It also reduces personnel risk in high-threat environments.

Strategic Impact

The strategic implications of the Department of Defense (DoD) potentially 'sacrificing' traditional 'exquisite' weapons systems in favor of low-cost autonomous weapons are profound and multi-faceted, extending across deterrence, force structure, industrial base, geopolitical dynamics, and ethical considerations.

I. Impact on Deterrence:

  • Credibility of Deterrence: A shift towards autonomous systems could enhance the credibility of deterrence by signaling an adversary's inability to achieve decisive victory through attrition. The sheer volume and expendability of low-cost drones can create a perception of 'unlimited' capacity to absorb losses and sustain combat operations, thereby complicating an adversary's warfighting calculus.
  • Cost Imposition: This strategy allows the U.S. to impose disproportionate costs on adversaries. Forcing a peer competitor to expend a multi-million dollar air-to-air missile or surface-to-air missile against a drone costing tens of thousands significantly degrades their high-end munitions stockpiles and strains their defense budget, making conflict economically unsustainable for them.
  • Perception of Superiority: While traditional systems project technological and qualitative superiority, a focus on autonomous mass projects adaptability, resilience, and a different form of technological advantage. The challenge lies in managing the perception among allies and adversaries – ensuring that a reduction in exquisite platforms is not misinterpreted as a weakening of overall conventional capabilities.

II. Transformation of Force Structure and Operational Doctrine:

  • Distributed Lethality and Resilience: The transition enables a more distributed, resilient, and adaptive force structure. Instead of concentrating capabilities in a few high-value targets, power is disaggregated across numerous nodes, making the overall system harder to degrade or neutralize. This enhances survivability in contested environments, particularly in Anti-Access/Area Denial (A2/AD) zones.
  • Operational Concepts: Doctrine would evolve from platform-centric operations to network-centric and data-centric warfare. This entails a greater emphasis on decentralized command and control, human-machine teaming, and artificial intelligence-driven decision support for orchestrating large-scale autonomous operations.
  • Personnel and Training: Significant changes would be required in military education, training, and recruitment. The demand for software engineers, AI specialists, data scientists, and robotics experts would increase, potentially shifting the composition of the uniformed services and their skill sets. Traditional maintenance and operational roles would also evolve to manage and control autonomous fleets rather than individually operating exquisite platforms.
  • Logistics and Maintenance: Autonomous systems, particularly smaller ones, may simplify logistics due to lighter weight, modularity, and potentially simpler manufacturing techniques (e.g., additive manufacturing at forward bases). However, it introduces new challenges in managing vast fleets, ensuring software updates, cybersecurity, and potentially establishing distributed manufacturing capabilities closer to the battlespace.

III. Impact on the Defense Industrial Base:

  • Shift in Contractor Landscape: The pivot could disrupt the traditional defense industrial base, which is heavily geared towards large, multi-decade programs for exquisite systems. Major prime contractors would need to adapt by developing expertise in agile software development, AI, robotics, and high-volume, lower-cost manufacturing.
  • Emergence of New Players: Smaller technology companies, startups, and even commercial entities specializing in AI, robotics, and commercial drone technology would gain prominence, fostering a more diverse and competitive defense ecosystem. This could lead to faster innovation cycles and a reduction in vendor lock-in.
  • Supply Chain Resilience: A focus on low-cost, often commercial-off-the-shelf (COTS) components, could diversify supply chains but also introduce new vulnerabilities related to foreign dependencies and intellectual property rights. The need for secure and trusted components would remain paramount.

IV. Geopolitical and Alliance Ramifications:

  • Alliance Interoperability: Integrating new autonomous capabilities with allies who may not have similar procurement strategies or technological readiness levels could pose challenges. Maintaining interoperability across diverse military architectures would be critical for coalition warfare.
  • Arms Race Dynamics: A significant U.S. investment in autonomous systems could accelerate an international arms race in this domain, prompting adversaries and potential competitors to intensify their own AI and robotics programs. This necessitates careful diplomacy and strategic communication to manage escalation risks.
  • Technology Transfer and Export Control: The dual-use nature of many autonomous technologies would complicate export control regimes. Balancing the need to share technology with allies for collective security against the risk of proliferation to adversaries would be a continuous challenge.

V. Ethical and Legal Considerations:

  • Lethal Autonomous Weapons Systems (LAWS): The expanded reliance on autonomous weapons brings intensified scrutiny to the ethical and legal frameworks governing their use. Debates over 'meaningful human control,' accountability for unintended consequences, and the potential for algorithmic bias in targeting would necessitate robust policy development and international dialogue.
  • Rules of Engagement: Existing rules of engagement may require significant adaptation to account for autonomous decision-making, particularly in complex or ambiguous combat situations. The integration of AI into targeting processes demands stringent validation and verification protocols.

Conclusion

The declaration by the Department of Defense's Chief Technology Officer, Emil Michael, regarding the potential 'sacrifice' of traditional, 'exquisite' weapons systems in favor of low-cost autonomous platforms represents far more than a mere budgetary adjustment; it signals a fundamental reconsideration of U.S. defense strategy in an era defined by intensifying Great Power Competition, rapid technological evolution, and complex fiscal realities. This strategic inflection point is driven by the imperative to maintain a decisive military advantage against adversaries who are increasingly leveraging asymmetric approaches and advanced technologies to challenge conventional superiority.

The detailed analysis herein underscores that such a trade-off, particularly in the context of potential budgetary constraints resulting from legislative 'reconciliation failing,' would profoundly reshape the U.S. military. It necessitates a pivot from a paradigm of concentrated, highly specialized, and inherently expensive platforms to one emphasizing distributed, resilient, and numerically superior forces empowered by advanced autonomy. While exquisite systems will likely retain a role in specific, high-end mission sets where their unique capabilities are indispensable, the center of gravity for future force generation appears poised to shift towards mass-producible, adaptable, and cost-effective autonomous solutions.

The implications for deterrence are significant, potentially establishing a new form of credible dissuasion based on the ability to impose unsustainable costs on an adversary through sheer volume and expendability. However, this transition is not without its complexities, demanding comprehensive reforms across force structure, operational doctrine, personnel development, and the defense industrial base. Furthermore, the ethical and legal challenges associated with expanding autonomous capabilities must be addressed proactively through robust policy frameworks and international engagement.

In conclusion, the path forward requires deliberate and sustained strategic planning. The DoD must skillfully navigate this transition, balancing the continued efficacy of its legacy capabilities with aggressive investment in future-oriented technologies. This involves fostering a culture of rapid innovation, cultivating new industrial partnerships, and rigorously evaluating the tactical and strategic effectiveness of autonomous systems in diverse operational scenarios. Ultimately, the success of this strategic reorientation will determine the U.S.'s ability to sustain its national security interests and maintain its competitive edge in the evolving global security landscape.
