Poland intends to buy two more squads of F-35s, minister says

Published: Fri, 12 Jun 2026 17:36:07 +0000

Executive Summary

This intelligence report assesses the strategic implications of the Republic of Poland's declared intent to double its acquisition of F-35 Lightning II fifth-generation multirole fighter aircraft. The proposed expansion entails an increase from the previously ordered 32 aircraft to a total of 64, effectively quadrupling Poland's fifth-generation air combat capability. This substantial procurement underscores Poland's resolute commitment to modernizing its air force, fortifying its defensive posture along NATO's eastern flank, and enhancing interoperability within the Alliance. The decision reflects a calculated response to evolving regional security dynamics, aiming to establish a credible deterrence capability and project significant air power. While this acquisition promises a transformative leap in operational capacity, it simultaneously necessitates considerable long-term financial investment, extensive infrastructure development, and a comprehensive human capital strategy to effectively integrate and sustain a fleet of this advanced magnitude.

Technical Deep-Dive

The F-35 Lightning II, developed under the Joint Strike Fighter (JSF) program, represents the pinnacle of contemporary military aviation technology, embodying a strategic shift towards fifth-generation air combat capabilities. Poland's focus is predominantly on the F-35A conventional take-off and landing (CTOL) variant, tailored for traditional runway operations.

1. Design and Stealth Characteristics: The F-35A's airframe is meticulously engineered for very low observable (VLO) stealth, crucial for penetrating advanced anti-access/area denial (A2/AD) environments. This is achieved through a combination of radar-absorbent materials (RAM), precise shaping that deflects radar waves, internal weapons bays to avoid external carriage radar reflections, and a sophisticated antenna farm integrated flush with the airframe. The reduced radar cross-section (RCS) significantly enhances survivability against modern surface-to-air missile (SAM) systems and advanced adversary fighter aircraft. Its aerodynamic design balances stealth with maneuverability, allowing for sustained supersonic flight (limited supercruise capability) and robust dogfighting performance, albeit not designed as an air-superiority 'dogfighter' in the traditional sense, but rather a networked sensor-shooter.

2. Propulsion System: At the core of the F-35A's power is the Pratt & Whitney F135-PW-100 turbofan engine. This formidable engine delivers approximately 43,000 pounds of thrust with afterburner, providing the aircraft with exceptional acceleration, climb rate, and overall performance. The F135 engine incorporates advanced materials and digital engine control for enhanced reliability, fuel efficiency, and reduced maintenance burden, critical for high operational tempo. Its robust design contributes to the aircraft's ability to carry substantial payloads and operate effectively in diverse environmental conditions.

3. Advanced Avionics and Sensor Fusion: The F-35's technological superiority is largely derived from its unparalleled sensor suite and the revolutionary concept of sensor fusion: * AN/APG-81 Active Electronically Scanned Array (AESA) Radar: This multi-function radar is a cornerstone of the F-35's combat effectiveness. It provides superior air-to-air target detection and tracking, high-resolution synthetic aperture radar (SAR) mapping for precision strike missions, ground moving target indication (GMTI) for tracking ground vehicles, and sophisticated electronic attack (EA) capabilities. The AESA architecture offers agility, redundancy, and low probability of intercept (LPI) characteristics. * Electro-Optical Targeting System (EOTS) AN/AAQ-40: Fully integrated beneath the nose, EOTS combines forward-looking infrared (FLIR), infrared search and track (IRST), and laser designator/rangefinder functionalities. This allows pilots to precisely identify, track, and engage air and ground targets from stealthy distances, even when radar emissions are undesirable. * Distributed Aperture System (DAS) AN/AAQ-37: Comprising six infrared cameras strategically placed around the airframe, DAS provides the pilot with a continuous, spherical, 360-degree view around the aircraft, projected directly onto the Helmet-Mounted Display System (HMDS). It detects and tracks aircraft and missiles from any direction, offers missile launch detection, provides superior navigation capabilities in degraded visual environments, and enhances situational awareness by removing blind spots, effectively serving as the pilot's 'eyes through the airframe'. * AN/ASQ-239 Barracuda Electronic Warfare (EW) Suite: This highly advanced EW system offers comprehensive electronic surveillance, countermeasures, and self-protection capabilities. It autonomously detects, identifies, and geolocates threats, and then can initiate precise electronic countermeasures, including sophisticated jamming techniques, to degrade or neutralize adversary radar and communications systems. Its tight integration with other sensors ensures a holistic view of the electromagnetic battlespace. * Helmet-Mounted Display System (HMDS): The F-35 pilot's HMDS replaces traditional head-up displays (HUDs) and night vision goggles. It projects all critical flight, sensor, and tactical information directly onto the pilot's visor, including the unparalleled DAS imagery. This provides intuitive situational awareness and precise targeting capabilities, enabling pilots to 'look through' the aircraft's floor to see targets or threats below. * Sensor Fusion Engine: The true revolution of the F-35 lies in its robust sensor fusion capabilities. Data from the APG-81 radar, EOTS, DAS, Barracuda EW suite, and off-board sources (e.g., Link 16, MADL) is continuously processed and synthesized by the aircraft's core processors. This creates a single, coherent, and highly accurate tactical picture, displayed intuitively to the pilot, significantly reducing cognitive load and enhancing decision-making speed in complex combat scenarios. This fused picture provides an unprecedented level of situational awareness, allowing the F-35 to operate as an airborne command and control node for networked operations.

4. Data Links and Networking: * Multi-function Advanced Data Link (MADL): A stealthy, high-bandwidth, directional data link designed specifically for F-35-to-F-35 communication. It enables secure, low-observable sharing of sensor data, target information, and tactical pictures between F-35s in a formation, facilitating cooperative engagements without compromising stealth. * Link 16: The F-35 also incorporates Link 16, ensuring interoperability with legacy 4th-generation aircraft and NATO assets, allowing it to share and receive non-stealthy tactical data with the broader coalition.

5. Armament: The F-35A features two internal weapons bays, crucial for maintaining its stealth profile. These bays can carry up to 2,500 pounds of ordnance, typically comprising two AIM-120 AMRAAM air-to-air missiles and two GBU-31/32/38 Joint Direct Attack Munitions (JDAMs) or GBU-12 Paveway II laser-guided bombs. Future upgrades will enable integration of additional weapons such as Joint Air-to-Surface Standoff Missile (JASSM) and Small Diameter Bombs (SDB). For missions where stealth is not paramount, the F-35A can utilize six external hardpoints, increasing its total weapons payload to 18,000 pounds, including various air-to-air, air-to-ground, and anti-ship munitions, though at the expense of its VLO characteristics ('beast mode'). It also features an internal GAU-22/A 25mm cannon for close-in air combat and strafing runs.

6. Logistics and Sustainment (ODIN): The F-35's sustainment model is uniquely complex, managed initially by the Autonomic Logistics Information System (ALIS), now transitioning to the Operational Data Integrated Network (ODIN). This global IT system provides prognostics, diagnostics, supply chain management, maintenance scheduling, and flight line operations support. It is designed to optimize fleet readiness by predicting maintenance needs and ensuring parts availability across the global F-35 enterprise. Doubling Poland's fleet to 64 aircraft will place a significantly increased demand on this system, requiring enhanced national infrastructure for maintenance, repair, and overhaul (MRO) capabilities, greater investment in spare parts pooling, and highly specialized technical personnel trained within the global F-35 ecosystem. The sheer volume of data generated by a larger fleet will also demand robust secure networks for data transfer and analysis.

7. Software and Block Upgrades: The F-35 is fundamentally a software-defined aircraft, with its capabilities largely determined by its continuously evolving software blocks. Poland's initial deliveries are expected to be Block 4 standard, offering enhanced processing power, expanded weapon carriage capabilities (e.g., SPEAR-3, JSM), and improved electronic warfare algorithms. The commitment to a larger fleet underscores the necessity of continuous software upgrades and associated training to leverage the aircraft's full potential throughout its operational lifespan, requiring a deep partnership with the F-35 Joint Program Office (JPO) and Lockheed Martin.

Impact of Doubling the Fleet (from 32 to 64 aircraft): * Enhanced Combat Mass and Persistent Presence: A fleet of 64 F-35s provides significantly greater combat mass, allowing for the deployment of larger packages for strike or air superiority missions, sustained air patrols over critical areas, and the ability to maintain higher sortie generation rates despite maintenance cycles. This directly translates to more credible deterrence and a greater capacity for protracted operations. * Operational Flexibility and Redundancy: A larger fleet permits greater flexibility in deploying aircraft to multiple airbases, rotating them through maintenance schedules without significant loss of operational capability, and establishing training squadrons that do not detract from operational readiness. It also provides redundancy against attrition, whether from combat, accidents, or long-term maintenance. * Training and Doctrine Evolution: A fleet of this size facilitates the development of advanced F-35 tactics, techniques, and procedures (TTPs). It allows for more comprehensive and realistic training exercises, including large-force engagements (LFE) and complex joint operations with NATO allies, accelerating the transition to a fifth-generation operational doctrine for the entire Polish Air Force. This includes the establishment of robust simulator facilities and extensive pilot and maintainer training programs. * Increased Logistical Footprint: While the benefits are substantial, doubling the fleet proportionally increases the logistical burden. This encompasses a greater demand for spare parts, consumables (fuel, munitions), specialized ground support equipment, and a significant expansion of highly trained maintenance personnel and technical experts. Poland will need to invest heavily in its national supply chain, repair facilities, and potentially seek deeper involvement in the F-35 global sustainment enterprise. * Infrastructure Expansion: Base infrastructure at designated F-35 operational locations will require substantial upgrades, including specialized hangars for stealth maintenance, secure mission planning facilities, hardened aircraft shelters, specialized fuel storage, munitions bunkers, and secure data network infrastructure to support ODIN and classified operations. This is a multi-billion dollar undertaking alongside the aircraft procurement cost itself.

In essence, the expansion to 64 F-35s moves Poland beyond a token fifth-generation capability to establish a formidable, enduring, and self-sufficient air combat force capable of shaping regional security outcomes.

Strategic Impact

The Republic of Poland's decision to pursue a doubling of its F-35 Lightning II acquisition holds profound strategic implications, positioning the nation as a pivotal actor within NATO's eastern flank and significantly reshaping the regional security calculus.

1. Enhanced Deterrence and Regional Security: * Credible Deterrence Against Aggression: A fleet of 64 F-35s provides Poland with a robust, multi-role air combat capability that serves as a powerful deterrent against potential aggressors, particularly Russia. The F-35's stealth, advanced sensors, and deep strike capabilities present a significant threat to adversary A2/AD systems and strategic targets, complicating any offensive planning. This enhanced capability makes the cost of aggression against Poland, and by extension NATO, demonstrably higher. * Strengthening NATO's Eastern Flank: As a frontline state, Poland's acquisition directly contributes to the collective defense posture of the Alliance. The F-35's ability to operate in highly contested airspace, gather intelligence, and share a fused tactical picture with other NATO assets enhances the overall situational awareness and combat effectiveness of the entire regional defense architecture. This investment signals Poland's unwavering commitment to burden-sharing within NATO. * Air Superiority and Control: The F-35's fifth-generation attributes will afford the Polish Air Force a significant advantage in achieving and maintaining air superiority over its adversaries. This dominance is critical for protecting ground forces, enabling strike operations, and ensuring the freedom of movement for coalition air assets.

2. NATO Interoperability and Alliance Cohesion: * Deepened Alliance Integration: With a substantial F-35 fleet, Poland further integrates into the growing community of NATO F-35 operators (e.g., USA, UK, Norway, Netherlands, Denmark, Belgium, Italy, Germany, Finland, Czech Republic). This commonality in platforms facilitates seamless interoperability, standardized tactics, techniques, and procedures (TTPs), and joint training exercises, enhancing the Alliance's ability to operate as a unified force. * Enhanced Data Sharing and Situational Awareness: The F-35's advanced data links (MADL, Link 16) and sensor fusion capabilities enable real-time, secure sharing of critical tactical intelligence across allied forces, improving collective situational awareness and decision-making speed in complex, multi-domain operations. Poland will become a significant node in this networked battlespace.

3. Modernization and Force Transformation of the Polish Armed Forces: * Acceleration of Legacy Platform Retirement: The F-35 acquisition accelerates the phasing out of older, Soviet-era aircraft (e.g., MiG-29, Su-22), completing the modernization of the Polish Air Force towards a predominantly Western, fifth-generation enabled fleet. This marks a fundamental transformation in Polish military doctrine and operational philosophy. * Shift to Network-Centric Warfare: The F-35's inherent networking capabilities, particularly its role as an advanced sensor node, will drive a broader transformation across the Polish Armed Forces towards network-centric warfare principles, integrating air, land, and naval assets into a more cohesive and responsive combat system.

4. Geopolitical and Economic Considerations: * Leadership in Central and Eastern Europe: This significant investment reinforces Poland's position as a leading military power and a key security provider in Central and Eastern Europe, potentially influencing other regional states to accelerate their own military modernization efforts. * Long-Term Financial Commitment: The procurement and sustainment of 64 F-35s represents a multi-decade financial commitment, encompassing not only the acquisition cost (tens of billions of USD) but also substantial investments in infrastructure, training, logistics, and ongoing modernization through block upgrades. This commitment will require sustained national resolve and potentially impact other defense sector procurements. * Industrial Participation: While primarily an off-the-shelf procurement, the scale of the order may open opportunities for Polish defense industries to participate in MRO activities, specialized component production, or software development, fostering domestic technological advancement and skilled job creation.

5. Challenges and Risks: * Human Capital Development: The specialized nature of F-35 operations demands a highly skilled workforce of pilots, maintainers, and support personnel. Recruiting, training, and retaining such talent will be a significant and ongoing challenge. * Logistical Complexity and Sustainment Costs: The F-35's global supply chain and high operational costs necessitate careful planning to ensure readiness rates. Poland will be highly reliant on the F-35 Joint Program Office and Lockheed Martin for critical spare parts and technical support, requiring robust national logistics and dedicated funding. * Cybersecurity Vulnerabilities: As a highly networked and software-dependent platform, the F-35 fleet presents a significant cybersecurity attack surface. Protecting the aircraft's systems, operational data, and logistical networks from sophisticated state-sponsored cyber threats will be paramount.

In summation, this expanded F-35 procurement is a transformative strategic decision for Poland, providing unparalleled air power capabilities and reinforcing its vital role within the NATO alliance, though demanding sustained national effort and resource allocation to fully realize its potential.

Conclusion

The Republic of Poland's declared intent to double its F-35 Lightning II procurement, escalating the order from 32 to 64 aircraft, signifies a monumental commitment to national defense and a profound transformation of its air power capabilities. This strategic decision will position the Polish Air Force as a dominant fifth-generation air combat force within NATO's eastern flank, offering unparalleled advantages in stealth, sensor fusion, and networked operations.

The technical superiority of the F-35, with its advanced avionics, comprehensive sensor suite, and sophisticated low-observable design, will provide Poland with a decisive edge in air superiority, precision strike, and intelligence, surveillance, and reconnaissance (ISR) missions. The expanded fleet size will generate critical combat mass, enhance operational flexibility, and accelerate the transition towards a modern, network-centric fighting force.

Strategically, this acquisition dramatically strengthens Poland's deterrent posture against regional threats, substantially enhances its interoperability and integration within the NATO alliance, and solidifies its leadership role in Central and Eastern European security. While the benefits are extensive, the financial, logistical, and human capital investments required to fully integrate and sustain a fleet of this magnitude are considerable and will demand sustained national focus and resource allocation over decades. Nevertheless, this ambitious modernization program underscores Poland's unwavering resolve to secure its national interests and contribute robustly to collective security, marking a new era for the Polish Armed Forces.
